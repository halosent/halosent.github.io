The Problem of Evil

If God is good and all powerful, why does suffering exist. The strongest form of the objection, and where it actually breaks down.

The argument usually starts with God defined as all knowing, all powerful, and all good. A God like that wouldn't allow unnecessary, gratuitous suffering. That doesn't mean suffering wouldn't exist. Not necessarily. Because moral standards have to come from somewhere. Without the possibility of real wrongdoing, genuine freedom isn't possible. And "good" stops meaning anything.

This argument assumes a good God would always eliminate suffering. That's not how it has to work. Eliminating every possibility of wrongdoing and evil eliminates free will along with it. If there's no possibility of choosing wrongly, there's no meaningful distinction between choosing good and choosing evil.

Therefore, for free will to exist. There has to be an option to choose wrongly. Because without that option. Free will fails to exist too.

If objective morality is real, that objectiveness needs a standard. Humans can't be that standard. We're all different, we disagree constantly, across cultures and across lifetimes. Which points to a higher being. What we call God.

Atheism can describe suffering. It struggles to explain why suffering is wrong rather than merely undesirable. Plenty of atheists hold to objective moral realism anyway. That still leaves the same gap. Something has to ground the standard. It isn't humans.

Two Creation Accounts: Genesis 1 and Genesis 2

Skeptics point to two orders of creation in the opening chapters of Genesis as proof of an editorial mistake. The genres involved say otherwise.

Genesis 1 lays out six days, ending with man and woman created together. Genesis 2 slows down on day six specifically. Man formed first, then animals, then the woman. Read as two attempts at the same timeline, and it looks like a contradiction. Read as two different jobs, and the tension mostly disappears.

Genesis 1 is a structured overview. Built around a deliberate pattern of forming and filling, which ancient audiences would have recognized as literary structure, not a strict timeline. Genesis 2 narrows in on the garden specifically. It's interested in something different, how humanity relates to the earth, to each other, and to God. There's also a grammar point worth knowing. The Hebrew in 2:19, usually translated God "formed" the animals, can just as legitimately be read as a pluperfect, "had formed." Which removes the sequencing problem entirely.

This isn't a rescue invented after the fact. Ancient Near Eastern writing regularly paired a broad cosmic account with a closer, thematic retelling of the same events. Genesis 1 and 2 fit that pattern. Think of it less like two competing witness statements, and more like a wide shot followed by a close up.

Judas' Death: Hanged, or Fell Headlong

Matthew says Judas hanged himself. Acts describes him falling and bursting open. Both can be true, and probably are.

Matthew says Judas hanged himself. Acts says he fell headlong, and his body burst open. Read as two full descriptions of the same moment, they don't match. Read as two partial glimpses of the same event, they line up. If grimly.

This isn't a new harmonization. People were proposing it long before modern skepticism came along. Judas hanged himself from a tree above the Field of Blood. At some point the rope or branch gave way, sending the body down onto rocky ground, where it split open. Matthew records how he died. Acts, written by Luke, records what was found afterward. Neither writer claims to be giving the whole picture. Which means neither excludes the other.

There's a detail people skip past. The Greek in Acts 1:18 doesn't strictly say Judas performed the fall himself. It can be read as "he came to his end there," which fits a body that was already dead by hanging before it came down. Two witnesses describing different stages of the same event is a far simpler explanation than a fabricated legend. Because simpler explanations tend to be the true ones.

The Two Genealogies of Christ

Matthew and Luke trace different fathers back from Joseph. First century genealogical convention explains why.

Matthew traces Jesus back through Solomon. Luke traces him back through Nathan, a different son of David. The names in between don't match at all. Critics call it a fabrication and move on. The oldest Christian answer is simpler. The two writers were never trying to trace the same line.

Matthew is writing for a Jewish audience, building a legal case for kingship. So he follows the royal succession through Solomon, exactly the record a first century reader would expect for a throne claim. Luke is after something else, an actual line of physical descent. The early historian Africanus records a tradition, older than any modern critic, that Luke's genealogy runs through Mary. With Joseph listed under her line, because Jewish records were kept patrilineally.

You don't need to land on one exact mechanism to see the pattern was normal for the period. Jewish genealogies routinely served legal or thematic purposes rather than strict biology. Skipping names, or following adopted lines, when the point called for it. Two lists disagreeing on names is only a contradiction if both are claiming to do the same job. Neither Gospel says they are.

Free Will and Divine Foreknowledge

If God already knows what you will choose tomorrow, is the choice actually yours. A distinction most debates skip past too quickly.

The objection sounds airtight the first time you hear it. If God already knows for certain you'll pick coffee tomorrow, then it's already fixed that you will. Which sounds like it erases any real freedom to choose otherwise. It's a fair objection. But it rests on mixing up two different things. Certainty and causation.

Knowing something happens isn't the same as causing it. Watch someone freely order coffee over tea, and your knowing what they chose didn't cause the choice. It just tracked one that already happened freely. St. Maximus the Confessor, and the broader patristic tradition, take this further. God isn't sitting inside our timeline, watching forward the way we do. He knows every moment, past, present, and future to us, in one single eternal act of knowing. Closer to how an author knows an entire story at once, rather than discovering the next page as it's written. That isn't prediction. It's eternity looking at genuinely free choices unfolding inside time.

This doesn't settle every related question. Grace, providence, and how much genuinely stays open are old, unresolved arguments, even within Orthodox thought. But the specific claim, that mere knowledge of a future choice cancels its freedom, doesn't hold up. Certainty about an outcome isn't the same as forcing that outcome. The objection only works if you collapse the two together.

Essence and Energies: How Orthodoxy Reads the Nature of God

The Palamite distinction is where a lot of East and West disagreement actually starts. Here is what it is and why it matters.

Scripture says two things about God that seem to pull against each other. No one sees God's essence and lives. And yet believers partake in the divine nature. Truly know God, rather than just knowing facts about him. St. Gregory Palamas, building on earlier Fathers, worked out the distinction that holds both together. God's essence, what he is in himself, stays forever inaccessible to any creature. God's energies, his uncreated activity and self disclosure, are how he's present and known. Not abstractly. Directly.

This isn't God on one side, and some lesser stand in on the other. The comparison the Fathers use is fire. The heat and light coming off it are still fully fire, not a diluted substitute. The energies are God acting. Not a created go between standing in the gap. Which is what lets the Church affirm real communion with God (theosis), without sliding into pantheism on one side, or backing into an unreachable deity on the other.

It matters for debate too, more than people expect. A lot of what gets labeled incoherent about classical theism, how a simple, unchanging God can still act, relate, and be experienced without changing, gets a real answer here instead of a dodge. I keep coming back to this one, because it isn't a patch bolted on to save the system. It's a worked out answer to a real problem, with centuries of careful thought behind it.

Is the Trinity Three Gods?

Father, Son, and Spirit, each called God, yet Scripture insists there is only one. The objection treats "person" and "being" as the same thing. They are not.

The Shema doesn't leave much room for interpretation. The Lord our God, the Lord is one. And yet the New Testament calls the Father God, calls the Son God, calls the Spirit God. While the Church has insisted from the start this isn't three deities. It's an easy objection to raise. But it assumes "person" and "being" mean the same thing. They don't.

The Cappadocian Fathers drew the distinction that does the real work here. Ousia, the one shared divine essence, and hypostasis, the distinct persons who each fully hold that one essence, without splitting it into thirds. This isn't a dodge dressed up in Greek. Three human beings share one human nature, and remain three distinct persons. Nobody calls that a contradiction. The difference with God is that the divine nature doesn't divide the way created natures do. So Father, Son, and Spirit each hold the whole of it, undivided, in relation to the other two.

Nothing here lands on three gods, because there aren't three beings to begin with. There's one being, existing eternally as three persons. It also doesn't collapse into one person wearing three masks, a separate error the Church rejected just as firmly, centuries ago. The doctrine is genuinely hard. And it should be. It's describing something with no exact parallel anywhere in creation. Hard and incoherent get confused constantly in this argument. They're not the same thing.

Who Incited David to Take the Census, God or Satan

2 Samuel says the Lord moved David to count Israel. Chronicles says Satan did. Both books can be right at once, and the reason why says something about how providence actually works.

2 Samuel says the Lord's anger moved David to count Israel. 1 Chronicles, telling the same story, says it was Satan. Read as two competing claims about who's responsible, it looks like a straightforward contradiction between two history books. Read as two levels of causation on the same event, it's fairly ordinary biblical language.

Scripture does this elsewhere too. Describes God permitting, or standing behind, something a hostile agent carries out. Without making God the author of the evil itself. Israel had sinned, and 2 Samuel frames what follows as judgment God allowed to unfold through Satan tempting David. The same structure you see in Job, where Satan does the acting, and God is the one who permits it within limits he sets. Chronicles, written later, and more interested in naming exactly who tempted David, just names the closer cause instead of the one standing behind it.

It reads less like a contradiction, and more like two focal lengths pointed at the same scene. One book showing the judgment behind it. The other naming the tempter inside it. Ancient Israelite writers didn't treat divine permission and human agency as mutually exclusive things that had to be reported one at a time. There's no reason a modern reader has to either.

How Many Women Were at the Tomb

Matthew names two, Mark names three, Luke leaves it open, John names one. This is exactly what independent eyewitness testimony looks like.

Matthew names two women. Mark adds a third. Luke leaves the count open. John narrows it down to one. Skeptics treat this as proof the resurrection accounts can't agree on a basic headcount. Let alone the bigger claim.

None of the four Gospels claims to be giving a complete list. Once you notice that, the "discrepancy" mostly stops looking like one. Each writer names whoever mattered to the part of the story he's telling. John's narrow focus on Mary Magdalene makes sense once you see he's tracking her specific movements, running to tell Peter and John, rather than cataloguing everyone present. That's what independent recollection of a chaotic morning tends to look like. Different people noticing and mentioning different things.

If all four accounts had listed the exact same names, in the exact same order, that would be more suspicious. More like one coordinated story than several people remembering the same event from their own angle. Historians and legal scholars generally treat this kind of partial overlap as a sign of authenticity, not a flaw. Which, if you think about how memory actually works, tracks.

Does God Change His Mind

Numbers says God does not change his mind like a man. Genesis says he regretted making humanity. Both are true, and the difference is what kind of language each one is using.

Numbers 23:19 says flatly that God isn't a man, that he shouldn't change his mind. Genesis 6:6 says he regretted making humanity. Exodus 32:14 says he relented from the disaster he'd promised. Put those next to each other, and one of them looks like it's ruling out exactly what the other two describe.

The classical answer leans on divine immutability, plus a careful look at what these passages are doing. Numbers is making a point about God's faithfulness to his promises, regardless of circumstance. That's what "doesn't change his mind" is guarding. The regret and relenting language in Genesis and Exodus describes God's unchanging character responding differently to changed human behavior. Not unlike a just judge handing down a different verdict to someone who's genuinely turned around. Without the judge himself becoming a different person in the process.

Scripture leans on human language, anger, regret, relenting, so finite readers can grasp how God's unchanging holiness meets a world that does change. That isn't evasive. It's a real theological distinction, between God's essence, which can't change because it isn't made of parts that could rearrange, and his relational posture toward creatures acting in time, which looks different depending on what they do.

Can God's Existence Be Proven? The Argument from Contingency

Not a proof in the mathematical sense, but a serious argument that the universe cannot be its own explanation. Where it succeeds and where it needs care.

Everything you run into in the universe is contingent. It exists, but didn't have to, and depends on something else to explain why it's here at all. You depend on your parents. The solar system depends on gravity behaving the way it does. Even the universe as a whole, if it's just a very large collection of contingent things, looks like the kind of thing that needs an explanation for why it exists. Rather than nothing existing instead.

The argument from contingency says that chain of contingent explanations can't regress forever. Not without eventually landing on something that isn't contingent. Something that exists necessarily, by what it is, rather than depending on anything further. Because if not. You never reach an explanation, just an endless hand off. Classical theism calls that necessary thing God. Not one more item sitting inside the universe, but the ground the universe's own existence rests on.

Fair caveat. Even granting the argument works, it lands you at a necessary, uncaused foundation of reality. Not automatically the personal, triune God of Christian revelation. Getting there takes more argument, and eventually revelation. What it does establish is that "why does anything exist at all" is a real question carrying real weight. And "the universe just is" isn't obviously a more reasonable answer than "something necessary grounds it."

The Witnesses to the Resurrection: What the Evidence Can and Can't Show

Paul says 500 people saw the risen Jesus at once. No source outside the Bible confirms it. Here's an honest look at what that objection does and doesn't prove.

Paul writes that the risen Jesus appeared to more than 500 people at once. He adds that most of them were still alive when he wrote it down. Skeptics point out, correctly, that nothing outside the New Testament confirms this happened. That's a fair observation.

Here's the thing though. "No outside corroboration" describes a huge share of what historians otherwise treat as settled about the ancient world. Most of what's accepted about Hannibal or Alexander the Great rests on a single surviving tradition, written decades or centuries later, drawing on earlier sources that no longer exist. Paul's letter is unusually early by comparison. Written maybe two decades out. The creed embedded inside it is dated by plenty of scholars, including ones with no religious stake in the outcome, to within a few years of the crucifixion itself.

It's also worth asking what kind of record would have caught this, even if it happened exactly as described. Roman administration wasn't logging the religious experiences of a few hundred provincial Galileans. Rome doesn't mention Christians at all until decades later. And even then, only in passing, in the context of unrest or executions. Not to weigh in on specific claims the movement was making about itself. Expecting a surviving Roman record here means expecting a kind of documentation that didn't exist for anyone, in this period, for events like this.

None of that turns the claim into proof. What's left is one early, specific source naming living witnesses. With nothing independently confirming it, and nothing independently contradicting it either. That's thinner evidence than a Roman census record would be. I think it's honest to treat it that way. One data point weighed alongside everything else. Not a knockdown argument on its own.

Is the God of the Old Testament Different from the God of the New Testament

Wrath and judgment on one side, love and mercy on the other. The contrast is real, but it isn't the split it first appears to be.

This one comes up constantly, and it isn't a bad faith objection. The Old Testament has conquest narratives, harsh judgments, commands that are genuinely hard to sit with. The New Testament centers a Jesus who preaches loving your enemies. At a glance, it can look like two different characters sharing a name.

The pattern falls apart once you read past the highlight reel of either testament. The Old Testament is loaded with tenderness. "As a father shows compassion to his children, so the Lord shows compassion," straight out of Psalm 103. Or the whole book of Hosea, built around God chasing after an unfaithful people. The New Testament isn't short on judgment either. Jesus talks about hell more than anyone else in it. Flips tables in the temple. Revelation describes judgment in some of the starkest language in the whole Bible. Both testaments hold love and justice together the entire time. They just shift the emphasis, depending on where you are in the story.

Both testaments read as one continuous unfolding of who God is. Not a character swap partway through. The severity in certain Old Testament passages sits inside a specific covenant moment, God forming and protecting a particular people at a particular point in the story. While the New Testament fills in the picture that was there the whole time. I won't pretend that fully dissolves the discomfort some of these passages create. It doesn't. Not every hard question here gets a tidy answer.

Did Jesus Ever Actually Claim to Be God

He never says the exact words "I am God" in the Gospels. The claim shows up in other ways, and the people around him clearly understood it.

It's true. Jesus never says the literal sentence "I am God" in any Gospel. Some people take that as proof the claim to divinity got added later by his followers. The stronger evidence runs the other way. Through what he actually did, and how the people standing right there reacted to it.

In Mark 2, Jesus tells a paralyzed man his sins are forgiven. The religious leaders in the room immediately object, since only God can forgive sins. Jesus doesn't walk that back. He heals the man specifically, to back up the authority they're objecting to. In John 8:58 he says "before Abraham was, I am," lifting God's own self identification to Moses word for word. The crowd responds by reaching for stones, the standard reaction to blasphemy under the law. At his trial in Mark 14, asked point blank whether he's the Christ, he answers with language pulled from Daniel 7. Which the entire Sanhedrin treats as blasphemy worth killing him over.

None of that needs the exact phrase "I am God" to make the point. The people in the room, who understood their own law and scripture far better than most modern readers do, reacted every time as though he was making exactly that claim. And reacted like it mattered enough to kill over. Whether the claim was true is a separate question from whether it was made. I think the case that it was made is strong. Whatever conclusion you draw from it after that.

Can Morality Exist Without God

Plenty of atheists live good, moral lives, that was never really the question. The harder question is what grounds objective moral obligation in the first place.

This question gets misheard almost every time it's raised. So it's worth being precise before going further. Nobody serious argues atheists can't live moral lives. Obviously they can, often admirably so. The real question sits one level down. What makes a claim like "torturing children for fun is wrong" objectively true. True regardless of what any individual or culture happens to believe. Rather than just a strongly shared preference.

The moral argument says objective moral values and duties are real. And that a purely naturalistic picture, matter and energy running on physical law with nothing further behind it, has a hard time grounding that kind of objectivity. Evolution can explain why humans developed moral instincts that helped the species survive together. Explaining why we feel that pull is different from explaining why the claim is objectively true, independent of anyone's feelings about it. Theism grounds moral obligation in the character of a necessarily existing, perfectly good God. An actual foundation, rather than a description of what happened to help early humans cooperate.

This isn't uncontested, and it shouldn't be presented as though it is. Secular moral realists argue moral facts can be objectively true the same way mathematical facts are. No divine mind required. Others argue morality doesn't need that strong a foundation to still meaningfully guide how people live. Both are serious positions, with serious people behind them. What I'd push back on is naturalism getting to treat objective morality as a free starting assumption. Because it has to earn that ground too. Same as theism does.
