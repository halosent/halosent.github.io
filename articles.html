Metaphysics

The Problem of Evil

If God is good and all powerful, why does suffering exist. The strongest form of the objection, and where it actually breaks down.

The objection usually gets stated like this: an all good, all powerful, all knowing God would not allow gratuitous suffering. Suffering exists, and much of it looks gratuitous. Therefore such a God does not exist. It is a real argument and it deserves a real answer, not a shrug.

The first thing to notice is that the argument needs a premise it never defends: that a good God would eliminate suffering rather than permit it for the sake of something greater, namely creaturely freedom and the possibility of real love. A world where love is possible is a world where rejection of love is also possible, and rejection has consequences that ripple outward. Remove the possibility of rejection and you remove the possibility of freely given love along with it. That is not a small trade.

The second problem is deeper. To call anything evil at all, gratuitous or not, is to measure it against a standard of how things ought to be. Where does that standard come from on a purely materialist account of the universe. Atheism can describe suffering, but it struggles to explain why suffering is wrong rather than merely unpleasant. Orthodox theology has an answer here: creation has a purpose and a good it is meant toward, and evil is a real privation of that good, not an equal and opposite force. The very fact that the problem of evil feels like an argument against God is itself evidence that we expect the world to be otherwise, which is a strange thing to expect in a universe with no author.

None of this makes suffering easy to sit with. It is not meant to. What it does is show that the philosophical argument, taken as a proof that God cannot exist, fails. The emotional weight of suffering is a separate and much harder pastoral question, and Christ's own answer to it was not an argument. It was to enter into it.

Contradiction

Two Creation Accounts: Genesis 1 and Genesis 2

Skeptics point to two orders of creation in the opening chapters of Genesis as proof of an editorial mistake. The genres involved say otherwise.

Genesis 1 gives a sweeping, ordered account of six days, ending with the creation of man and woman together. Genesis 2 zooms in on day six and narrates man being formed first, then the animals, then the woman. Read as two competing timelines, this looks like a contradiction. Read as two texts doing different jobs, it is not one.

Genesis 1 is structured as a cosmic overview, arranged in a deliberate literary pattern of forming and filling, day paired against day, that ancient readers would have recognized as a genre choice rather than a strict chronological log. Genesis 2 is a close narrative focused entirely on the garden, written to explain humanity's relationship to the earth, to each other, and to God. The Hebrew of Genesis 2:19, often translated as God "formed" the animals, can just as legitimately be rendered as a pluperfect, "had formed," which removes the supposed sequence problem entirely and lines the two chapters back up.

This is not special pleading invented to save the text. Ancient Near Eastern literature regularly paired a broad cosmological account with a focused, thematic retelling covering the same events from a different angle, and Genesis 1 and 2 fit that pattern precisely. The two chapters are not competing sources stitched together carelessly. They are a wide shot followed by a close up, the way a film might open with a skyline before cutting to one apartment window.

Contradiction

Judas' Death: Hanged, or Fell Headlong

Matthew says Judas hanged himself. Acts describes him falling and bursting open. Both can be true, and probably are.

Matthew 27:5 says Judas went out and hanged himself. Acts 1:18 says he fell headlong, his body burst open, and his insides spilled out. Taken as two full descriptions of the same moment, they look irreconcilable. Taken as two partial descriptions of the same event, they fit together in an unpleasant but coherent way.

The most natural harmonization, and one proposed long before the modern skeptical tradition existed, is that Judas hanged himself from a tree on a ledge or cliff above the Field of Blood, and that the branch broke or the rope gave way after death, causing the body to fall onto rocky ground below and rupture on impact. Matthew records the method. Luke, writing Acts, records the grim aftermath as it was discovered. Neither account claims to be exhaustive, and neither excludes the other.

It is also worth noting that Acts 1:18 does not actually claim Judas is the one who acted here. The Greek allows for the reading that he came to his end there, rather than performed the falling himself, which fits a scenario where the body was already dead by hanging before it came down. Two witnesses describing different stages of the same tragedy is a far more mundane explanation than a fabricated legend, and it is the one that best accounts for both texts as written.

Contradiction

The Two Genealogies of Christ

Matthew and Luke trace different fathers back from Joseph. First century genealogical convention explains why.

Matthew 1 traces Jesus' line through Solomon, David's son. Luke 3 traces it through Nathan, a different son of David entirely, and the names between David and Joseph do not match. Critics call this a straightforward fabrication. The oldest and most consistent Christian explanation is that the two evangelists are tracing two different lines on purpose.

Matthew writes for a Jewish audience and is establishing Jesus' legal right to David's throne, so he follows the royal line through Solomon, the succession of kings, exactly the record a first century reader would expect for a claim to kingship. Luke is working from a different concern, tracing an actual biological descent, and the early church historian Africanus, writing well before modern criticism existed, records a longstanding tradition that Luke's line runs through Mary rather than Joseph, with Joseph listed because Jewish record keeping was patrilineal and a son in law could be recorded under his father in law's line through levirate custom.

Whichever precise mechanism is correct, and there were multiple live proposals even among the earliest Christian writers, the pattern itself was not unusual. Jewish genealogies frequently served a legal or thematic purpose rather than a strict biological one, skipping generations or following adoptive and legal lines when the point being made called for it. Two genealogies disagreeing on names is only a contradiction if both are claimed to be doing the identical job, and nothing in either Gospel makes that claim.

Free Will and Divine Foreknowledge

If God already knows what you will choose tomorrow, is the choice actually yours. A distinction most debates skip past too quickly.

The objection sounds tight at first: if God infallibly knows now that you will choose X tomorrow, then it is already fixed that you will choose X, which sounds like it removes the freedom to choose otherwise. This confuses two very different things, certainty and causation.

Knowledge of an event is not the same as causing it. If I watch you freely choose coffee over tea this morning, my knowledge that you chose coffee does not cause the choice, it simply tracks a choice you made freely. The classical Orthodox and broader patristic answer, developed at length by writers like St. Maximus the Confessor, is that God does not stand inside our timeline looking forward the way we do. He knows all moments, past, present, and future to us, in a single eternal act of knowing, the way an author knows an entire story at once rather than discovering the next page as the reader turns it. Foreknowledge in that framework is not prediction. It is the vantage point of eternity looking at genuinely free acts happening in time.

This does not resolve every difficulty in the neighborhood, questions about grace, providence, and how much is truly left open are old and contested even within Orthodox thought. But the specific objection, that mere knowledge of a future free choice logically cancels its freedom, does not hold up. Certainty about an outcome and causal necessity of that outcome are not the same relation, and collapsing them together is where the argument actually fails.

Metaphysics

Essence and Energies: How Orthodoxy Reads the Nature of God

The Palamite distinction is where a lot of East and West disagreement actually starts. Here is what it is and why it matters.

Scripture claims two things about God that look, on the surface, hard to hold together. It says no one has seen God's essence and lived, and it says believers can genuinely partake in the divine nature and truly know God, not merely know facts about him. St. Gregory Palamas, drawing on earlier Fathers, formalized the distinction that resolves this: God's essence, what he is in himself, remains forever unknowable and inaccessible to any creature. God's energies, his uncreated activity and self disclosure in the world, are how he is genuinely present and truly experienced.

This is not a distinction between God and something less than God, the way heat and light coming off a fire are still fully fire and not some lesser substitute for it. The energies are fully God acting, not a created intermediary standing between God and the world. This lets Orthodox theology affirm real, direct communion with God, what the tradition calls theosis, without collapsing into pantheism on one side or a distant unreachable deity on the other.

It also carries real weight in debate. A great deal of what gets called incoherence in classical theism, how a simple, immutable God can act, relate, and be experienced without change or composition, gets addressed directly by this distinction rather than argued away. This is a large part of why I find these conversations more interesting than most contradiction lists. The essence and energies distinction is not a defensive patch. It is a serious answer to a serious metaphysical problem, and it has centuries of careful thought behind it.
