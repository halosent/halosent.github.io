The Problem of Evil

If God is good and all powerful, why does suffering exist. The strongest form of the objection, and where it actually breaks down.

The objection usually gets stated like this: an all good, all powerful, all knowing God would not allow gratuitous suffering. Suffering exists, and much of it looks gratuitous. Therefore such a God does not exist. It is a real argument and it deserves a real answer, not a shrug.

The first thing to notice is that the argument needs a premise it never defends: that a good God would eliminate suffering rather than permit it for the sake of something greater, namely creaturely freedom and the possibility of real love. A world where love is possible is a world where rejection of love is also possible, and rejection has consequences that ripple outward. Remove the possibility of rejection and you remove the possibility of freely given love along with it. That is not a small trade.

The second problem is deeper. To call anything evil at all, gratuitous or not, is to measure it against a standard of how things ought to be. Where does that standard come from on a purely materialist account of the universe. Atheism can describe suffering, but it struggles to explain why suffering is wrong rather than merely unpleasant. Orthodox theology has an answer here: creation has a purpose and a good it is meant toward, and evil is a real privation of that good, not an equal and opposite force. The very fact that the problem of evil feels like an argument against God is itself evidence that we expect the world to be otherwise, which is a strange thing to expect in a universe with no author.

None of this makes suffering easy to sit with. It is not meant to. What it does is show that the philosophical argument, taken as a proof that God cannot exist, fails. The emotional weight of suffering is a separate and much harder pastoral question, and Christ's own answer to it was not an argument. It was to enter into it.

Two Creation Accounts: Genesis 1 and Genesis 2

Skeptics point to two orders of creation in the opening chapters of Genesis as proof of an editorial mistake. The genres involved say otherwise.

Genesis 1 gives a sweeping, ordered account of six days, ending with the creation of man and woman together. Genesis 2 zooms in on day six and narrates man being formed first, then the animals, then the woman. Read as two competing timelines, this looks like a contradiction. Read as two texts doing different jobs, it is not one.

Genesis 1 is structured as a cosmic overview, arranged in a deliberate literary pattern of forming and filling, day paired against day, that ancient readers would have recognized as a genre choice rather than a strict chronological log. Genesis 2 is a close narrative focused entirely on the garden, written to explain humanity's relationship to the earth, to each other, and to God. The Hebrew of Genesis 2:19, often translated as God "formed" the animals, can just as legitimately be rendered as a pluperfect, "had formed," which removes the supposed sequence problem entirely and lines the two chapters back up.

This is not special pleading invented to save the text. Ancient Near Eastern literature regularly paired a broad cosmological account with a focused, thematic retelling covering the same events from a different angle, and Genesis 1 and 2 fit that pattern precisely. The two chapters are not competing sources stitched together carelessly. They are a wide shot followed by a close up, the way a film might open with a skyline before cutting to one apartment window.

Judas' Death: Hanged, or Fell Headlong

Matthew says Judas hanged himself. Acts describes him falling and bursting open. Both can be true, and probably are.

Matthew 27:5 says Judas went out and hanged himself. Acts 1:18 says he fell headlong, his body burst open, and his insides spilled out. Taken as two full descriptions of the same moment, they look irreconcilable. Taken as two partial descriptions of the same event, they fit together in an unpleasant but coherent way.

The most natural harmonization, and one proposed long before the modern skeptical tradition existed, is that Judas hanged himself from a tree on a ledge or cliff above the Field of Blood, and that the branch broke or the rope gave way after death, causing the body to fall onto rocky ground below and rupture on impact. Matthew records the method. Luke, writing Acts, records the grim aftermath as it was discovered. Neither account claims to be exhaustive, and neither excludes the other.

It is also worth noting that Acts 1:18 does not actually claim Judas is the one who acted here. The Greek allows for the reading that he came to his end there, rather than performed the falling himself, which fits a scenario where the body was already dead by hanging before it came down. Two witnesses describing different stages of the same tragedy is a far more mundane explanation than a fabricated legend, and it is the one that best accounts for both texts as written.

The Two Genealogies of Christ

Matthew and Luke trace different fathers back from Joseph. First century genealogical convention explains why.

Matthew 1 traces Jesus' line through Solomon, David's son. Luke 3 traces it through Nathan, a different son of David entirely, and the names between David and Joseph do not match. Critics call this a straightforward fabrication. The oldest and most consistent Christian explanation is that the two evangelists are tracing two different lines on purpose.

Matthew writes for a Jewish audience and is establishing Jesus' legal right to David's throne, so he follows the royal line through Solomon, the succession of kings, exactly the record a first century reader would expect for a claim to kingship. Luke is working from a different concern, tracing an actual biological descent, and the early church historian Africanus, writing well before modern criticism existed, records a longstanding tradition that Luke's line runs through Mary rather than Joseph, with Joseph listed because Jewish record keeping was patrilineal and a son in law could be recorded under his father in law's line through levirate custom.

Whichever precise mechanism is correct, and there were multiple live proposals even among the earliest Christian writers, the pattern itself was not unusual. Jewish genealogies frequently served a legal or thematic purpose rather than a strict biological one, skipping generations or following adoptive and legal lines when the point being made called for it. Two genealogies disagreeing on names is only a contradiction if both are claimed to be doing the identical job, and nothing in either Gospel makes that claim.

Free Will and Divine Foreknowledge

If God already knows what you will choose tomorrow, is the choice actually yours. A distinction most debates skip past too quickly.

The objection sounds tight at first: if God infallibly knows now that you will choose X tomorrow, then it is already fixed that you will choose X, which sounds like it removes the freedom to choose otherwise. This confuses two very different things, certainty and causation.

Knowledge of an event is not the same as causing it. If I watch you freely choose coffee over tea this morning, my knowledge that you chose coffee does not cause the choice, it simply tracks a choice you made freely. The classical Orthodox and broader patristic answer, developed at length by writers like St. Maximus the Confessor, is that God does not stand inside our timeline looking forward the way we do. He knows all moments, past, present, and future to us, in a single eternal act of knowing, the way an author knows an entire story at once rather than discovering the next page as the reader turns it. Foreknowledge in that framework is not prediction. It is the vantage point of eternity looking at genuinely free acts happening in time.

This does not resolve every difficulty in the neighborhood, questions about grace, providence, and how much is truly left open are old and contested even within Orthodox thought. But the specific objection, that mere knowledge of a future free choice logically cancels its freedom, does not hold up. Certainty about an outcome and causal necessity of that outcome are not the same relation, and collapsing them together is where the argument actually fails.

Essence and Energies: How Orthodoxy Reads the Nature of God

The Palamite distinction is where a lot of East and West disagreement actually starts. Here is what it is and why it matters.

Scripture claims two things about God that look, on the surface, hard to hold together. It says no one has seen God's essence and lived, and it says believers can genuinely partake in the divine nature and truly know God, not merely know facts about him. St. Gregory Palamas, drawing on earlier Fathers, formalized the distinction that resolves this: God's essence, what he is in himself, remains forever unknowable and inaccessible to any creature. God's energies, his uncreated activity and self disclosure in the world, are how he is genuinely present and truly experienced.

This is not a distinction between God and something less than God, the way heat and light coming off a fire are still fully fire and not some lesser substitute for it. The energies are fully God acting, not a created intermediary standing between God and the world. This lets Orthodox theology affirm real, direct communion with God, what the tradition calls theosis, without collapsing into pantheism on one side or a distant unreachable deity on the other.

It also carries real weight in debate. A great deal of what gets called incoherence in classical theism, how a simple, immutable God can act, relate, and be experienced without change or composition, gets addressed directly by this distinction rather than argued away. This is a large part of why I find these conversations more interesting than most contradiction lists. The essence and energies distinction is not a defensive patch. It is a serious answer to a serious metaphysical problem, and it has centuries of careful thought behind it.

Is the Trinity Three Gods?

Father, Son, and Spirit, each called God, yet Scripture insists there is only one. The objection treats "person" and "being" as the same thing. They are not.

The Shema is unambiguous. The Lord our God, the Lord is one. Yet the New Testament calls the Father God, calls the Son God, and calls the Spirit God, and the Church has always insisted this is not three deities. Skeptics take this as a plain contradiction, tritheism dressed up in monotheistic language. The objection only works if "person" and "being" mean the same thing, and they do not.

Orthodox theology, following the Cappadocian Fathers, distinguishes between ousia, the one shared divine essence or being, and hypostasis, the distinct persons who each fully possess that one essence without dividing it into three parts. This is not a trick of vocabulary invented to dodge the problem. Three human beings share one human nature, yet each is a distinct person, and nobody calls that a contradiction. The difference with God is that the divine nature is not divisible the way created natures are, so Father, Son, and Spirit do not each possess a third of it. Each possesses the whole of it, undivided, in eternal relation to the other two.

Nothing in this claims three gods, because there are not three beings, there is one being eternally existing as three persons. It also does not collapse into three names for one person, which is a separate ancient error the Church rejected just as firmly. The doctrine is difficult, and it should be, because it is describing something that has no exact parallel in creation. Difficult is not the same as incoherent, and the two get confused far too often in this particular argument.

Who Incited David to Take the Census, God or Satan

2 Samuel says the Lord moved David to count Israel. Chronicles says Satan did. Both books can be right at once, and the reason why says something about how providence actually works.

2 Samuel 24:1 says the Lord's anger against Israel moved David to take a census. 1 Chronicles 21:1, telling the same story, says Satan incited David to do it. Read as two exclusive claims about who pulled the strings, this looks like a flat contradiction between two historical books. Read as two levels of causation operating on the same event, it is standard biblical language rather than an error.

Scripture regularly describes God as permitting or standing behind an action that a hostile agent actually carries out, without treating God as the moral author of the evil involved. Israel had sinned, and 2 Samuel frames what follows as an act of judgment that God allowed to unfold through Satan's temptation of David, exactly the same pattern seen in Job, where Satan is the direct actor and God is the one who permits the test within limits he sets. Chronicles, written later with a sharper interest in exactly who tempted David personally, simply names the proximate cause instead of the permissive one.

This is not a contradiction so much as two focal lengths on the same event, one book describing the judgment behind it, the other naming the tempter within it. Ancient Israelite historiography did not treat divine permission and creaturely agency as competitors that needed to be reported as mutually exclusive, and neither should a modern reader trying to reconcile the two accounts.

How Many Women Were at the Tomb

Matthew names two, Mark names three, Luke leaves it open, John names one. This is exactly what independent eyewitness testimony looks like.

Matthew mentions Mary Magdalene and the other Mary. Mark adds Salome. Luke mentions Joanna alongside Mary Magdalene and Mary the mother of James, plus others left unnamed. John narrows the account to Mary Magdalene alone. Skeptics present this as proof the resurrection accounts cannot be trusted, four sources that cannot agree on a basic headcount.

None of the four Gospels claims to be giving an exhaustive list. Each writer names whoever was relevant to the part of the story he is telling, and a closer look shows every account is compatible with a larger group that each Gospel simply partially names. John's focus on Mary Magdalene alone fits naturally once you notice he is tracking her specific movements, running to tell Peter and John, rather than reporting on the whole group at once. This is precisely the kind of variation that shows up in real, independent testimony about a real event, where different witnesses foreground different people based on who they were paying attention to.

If all four accounts listed the identical names in the identical order, that uniformity would actually be more suspicious, evidence of a single coordinated story rather than separate testimony. Legal and historical scholars generally treat this kind of partial overlap as a mark of authenticity rather than a flaw, and the resurrection narratives read exactly like what they claim to be, several people recalling a chaotic early morning from their own vantage point.

Does God Change His Mind

Numbers says God does not change his mind like a man. Genesis says he regretted making humanity. Both are true, and the difference is what kind of language each one is using.

Numbers 23:19 states plainly that God is not a man, that he should change his mind. Genesis 6:6 says the Lord regretted making man on the earth, and Exodus 32:14 says the Lord relented from the disaster he had spoken of bringing on Israel. Set side by side, one text seems to rule out exactly what the other two describe happening.

The classical Orthodox answer rests on the doctrine of divine immutability alongside a careful reading of what these narrative texts are actually doing. God's essential nature and his ultimate purposes do not shift, that is the point Numbers is making, in a context specifically about God's faithfulness to his promises regardless of circumstances. The language of regret and relenting in Genesis and Exodus describes God's unchanging character responding differently to changed human circumstances, the way a judge who is unwaveringly just will hand down a different verdict to a defendant who genuinely repents, without the judge himself having become a different kind of person.

Scripture routinely describes God in human terms, anger, regret, relenting, so that finite readers can grasp how his unchanging holiness and justice intersect with a world that does change. This is not evasive. It reflects a considered theological distinction between God's eternal essence, which cannot change because it is not composed of parts that could rearrange, and his relational stance toward creatures acting within time, which appropriately looks different depending on what those creatures do.

Can God's Existence Be Proven? The Argument from Contingency

Not a proof in the mathematical sense, but a serious argument that the universe cannot be its own explanation. Where it succeeds and where it needs care.

Everything we encounter in the universe is contingent, meaning it exists but did not have to exist, and depends on something else for its existence. You depend on your parents, the solar system, and the laws of physics holding steady. Even the universe as a whole, if it is a collection of contingent things, looks like the kind of thing that needs an explanation for why it exists rather than nothing existing at all.

The argument from contingency holds that a chain of contingent explanations cannot regress forever without ultimately bottoming out in something that is not itself contingent, something that exists necessarily, by the nature of what it is, rather than depending on anything further. Otherwise you never actually arrive at an explanation, only an infinite deferral of one. That necessary being is what classical theism calls God, not as an extra item added to the universe, but as the ground that the universe's own existence depends on.

The honest caveat is that this argument, even if sound, gets you to a necessary, uncaused foundation of reality. It does not by itself get you all the way to the personal, triune God of Christian revelation, that requires further argument and, ultimately, revelation. What it does show is that the demand "why does anything at all exist" is a real philosophical question with real weight, and that the answer "the universe just is" is not obviously more reasonable than "something necessary grounds it." Whether atheism actually gets to claim the neutral, assumption free position here is itself worth debating, and I'd genuinely like to hear the case for either side.

The Witnesses to the Resurrection: What the Evidence Can and Can't Show

Paul says 500 people saw the risen Jesus at once. No source outside the Bible confirms it. Here's an honest look at what that objection does and doesn't prove.

1 Corinthians 15:6 says the risen Jesus appeared to more than 500 people at once, most of whom, Paul adds, were still alive at the time of writing. Skeptics point out, correctly, that no source outside the New Testament confirms this happened. That's a fair observation, and it deserves a fair answer rather than a dismissal.

It's worth noticing first that "no outside corroboration" describes an enormous amount of what historians otherwise treat as reasonably established about the ancient world. Most of what's accepted about Hannibal's campaigns or Alexander the Great rests on single traditions written decades or centuries after the fact, working from earlier sources that no longer survive. Paul's letter is unusually early by comparison, written roughly two decades after the event, and the creedal formula inside it is dated by many scholars, including skeptical ones, to within a few years of the crucifixion itself.

It's also worth asking what kind of record keeping would have caught this even if it happened exactly as described. Roman and Jewish administration of the period wasn't tracking the private religious experiences of a few hundred provincial Galileans. Rome only starts mentioning Christians at all decades later, and even then only in passing, in the context of unrest or executions, not to evaluate specific claims made within the movement. Expecting a surviving Roman record of this appearance is expecting a kind of documentation that simply didn't exist for events like this, for anyone, in this period.

None of this turns the claim into proof. What's left is a single, but early and specific, source making a claim to living, namable witnesses, with no independent confirmation and no independent contradiction either. That's genuinely a more modest kind of evidence than a Roman census record would be, and I think it's fair to treat it that way, as one data point that has to be weighed alongside everything else, rather than as a knockdown argument on its own. Where people land on it after that tends to depend on prior questions about testimony, history, and the miraculous more than on this specific passage.

Is the God of the Old Testament Different from the God of the New Testament

Wrath and judgment on one side, love and mercy on the other. The contrast is real, but it isn't the split it first appears to be.

This is one of the most common objections raised, and it's an understandable one. The Old Testament includes conquest narratives, harsh judgments, and commands that are genuinely difficult to read. The New Testament centers on a Jesus who teaches love of enemies and forgiveness. It can look like two different characters wearing the same name.

The pattern breaks down once you read past the surface of either testament. The Old Testament is full of tenderness: "as a father shows compassion to his children, so the Lord shows compassion," in Psalm 103, or the entire book of Hosea built around God's persistent love for an unfaithful people. The New Testament, meanwhile, is not short on judgment. Jesus speaks about hell more than anyone else in the New Testament, overturns tables in the temple, and the book of Revelation depicts divine judgment in stark terms. Both testaments hold love and justice together, they just distribute the emphasis differently depending on where a given passage sits in the story.

Orthodox theology reads the two testaments as one continuous narrative of God's self revelation, unfolding progressively rather than shifting into a different personality partway through. The severity of certain Old Testament passages sits inside a specific covenant context, God shaping and protecting a particular people at a particular moment in redemptive history, while the New Testament reveals the fuller picture that was there all along, that judgment and mercy were never actually in tension in the first place. Whether that framing fully resolves the discomfort some of these passages raise is a fair thing to keep discussing, and I don't think every hard question here gets a tidy answer.

Did Jesus Ever Actually Claim to Be God

He never says the exact words "I am God" in the Gospels. The claim shows up in other ways, and the people around him clearly understood it.

It's true that a sentence like "I am God" never appears on Jesus' lips in any Gospel. Some conclude from this that the claim to divinity was a later theological addition rather than something Jesus said about himself. The stronger evidence points the other way, through what he did and how his own audience reacted to it.

In Mark 2, Jesus tells a paralyzed man his sins are forgiven, and the religious leaders present immediately object, asking who can forgive sins but God alone. Jesus doesn't correct their theology, he heals the man specifically to demonstrate he has the authority they're objecting to. In John 8:58, Jesus says "before Abraham was, I am," directly echoing God's self identification to Moses in Exodus, and the crowd responds by picking up stones, the standard response to blasphemy under Jewish law. At his own trial in Mark 14, when the high priest asks directly whether he is the Christ, Jesus answers using language drawn from Daniel 7 that the Sanhedrin unanimously treats as blasphemy worthy of death.

None of this requires the exact phrase "I am God" to make the point. The people in the room, who knew their own scripture and law far better than a modern reader typically does, consistently reacted as though he was making exactly that claim, and reacted with lethal seriousness. Whether that claim was true is a separate question from whether it was made, and I think the historical case that it was made is genuinely strong, even for readers who land elsewhere on what to make of it.

Can Morality Exist Without God

Plenty of atheists live good, moral lives, that was never really the question. The harder question is what grounds objective moral obligation in the first place.

This question gets misheard constantly, so it's worth being precise about it upfront. Nobody serious is claiming atheists can't behave morally, plainly they can and do, often admirably. The actual question is a metaphysical one: what makes a moral claim like "torturing children for entertainment is wrong" objectively true, true regardless of what any individual or culture happens to believe, rather than simply a strong shared preference.

The moral argument holds that objective moral values and duties are real, and that a purely naturalistic universe, matter and energy operating under physical law with no further purpose behind it, struggles to ground that kind of objectivity. Evolution can explain why humans developed moral feelings that helped the species survive, but explaining why we feel a moral pull toward something is different from explaining why it's actually, objectively true, independent of anyone's feelings about it. Theism grounds moral obligation in the character of a necessarily existing, perfectly good God, giving objective moral facts an actual foundation rather than a description of what happened to help early humans cooperate.

This isn't uncontested, and it shouldn't be presented as though it is. Secular moral realists argue moral facts can be objectively true the same way mathematical facts are, without needing a divine mind behind them. Others argue morality doesn't need to be objective in that strong sense at all to still meaningfully guide how we live. Those are serious positions with serious defenders, and I don't think this question gets settled in a few paragraphs. What I'd push back on is the idea that naturalism gets to treat objective morality as a free, assumed starting point. It has to earn that ground too, just like theism does.
